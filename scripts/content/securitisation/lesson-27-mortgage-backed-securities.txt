A homeowner signs a thirty-year mortgage, but the investor who eventually funds that mortgage may not wait thirty years for every rupee. The loan can be prepaid when the family moves, refinanced when rates fall, modified after income loss, or delayed through foreclosure. Mortgage-backed securities begin with a simple promise to pay and become complicated when thousands of borrowers exercise different choices at different times.

Mortgage-backed securities, or M B S, are securities supported primarily by mortgage loans or their cash flows. Residential mortgages and commercial mortgages belong to the same broad family, but the property, borrower, lease, valuation, servicing, and default processes are different. A mortgage label tells you the collateral class. It does not tell you whether the investor receives a direct pass-through of borrower cash or a reshaped payment profile.

Take a pool of one thousand home loans. Borrowers make interest and principal payments. The servicer collects, reconciles, and remits the money. In a pass-through structure, after permitted deductions, principal and interest move more directly to investors. In a pay-through structure, the issuer may hold collections and use a waterfall to create different note maturities and priorities. The same mortgages can therefore produce different investor clocks.

The first clock is contractual maturity. A thirty-year mortgage may legally end in 2056. The second clock is expected cash return. If borrowers refinance in year four, principal returns much earlier. If rates rise and refinancing slows, the security extends. The investor is not merely asking whether the borrower will pay. The investor is asking when principal will return, what coupon will remain, and whether the returned money can be reinvested at the same yield.

Prepayment is not a footnote. A homeowner who sells a house has not defaulted, but early principal can still hurt an investor who bought a premium security expecting years of interest. Slow prepayment can extend duration just when rates are high and refinancing is unattractive. The Public Securities Association convention, commonly called P S A, gives analysts a common language for mortgage prepayment assumptions. It is a convention, not a promise made by borrowers.

Credit risk enters through another route. A borrower can become delinquent, request modification, or default. The servicer must contact the borrower, apply the contract and applicable rules, manage advances, protect the property, and eventually coordinate foreclosure or sale where permitted. A recovery percentage without recovery timing is incomplete. Forty percent received after three months is not economically the same as forty percent received after three years.

Mortgage securities also depend on the legal transfer. The investor needs to know whether the issuing vehicle owns the mortgage rights, whether the mortgage records and payment instructions support enforcement, whether collections are segregated, and what happens if the originator or servicer fails. Bankruptcy remoteness protects against a sponsor failure; it does not protect against a national fall in house prices or a wave of borrower unemployment.

Agency and non-agency structures make the distinction sharper. An agency-linked security may benefit from a government-sponsored or government guarantee framework, subject to the exact program and its terms. A non-agency security relies more directly on collateral performance, subordination, reserves, excess spread, insurance, and the transaction waterfall. The word agency is not a substitute for reading the guarantee, servicing, liquidity, and prepayment provisions.

The mortgage crisis shows how the family can fail in several places at once. A loan can be poorly underwritten before it reaches the pool. A model can understate correlation between borrowers. A servicer can modify loans in ways that change cash timing. A rating can describe a methodology rather than a guarantee. A liquid market can disappear even while borrowers continue paying. The security is a chain, and the chain can weaken at every link.

When comparing MBS with auto ABS, keep the difference visible. An auto pool usually amortises through scheduled instalments and vehicle recovery. A mortgage pool contains a valuable borrower option to prepay, property-value exposure, foreclosure law, insurance, tax, and much longer legal maturity. The investor needs a different model because the cash engine is different.

An interview-quality answer follows the payment. State the mortgage type, borrower or property, servicer, legal owner, pass-through or pay-through form, expected prepayment, default and recovery path, enhancement, trigger, and final recipient of cash. Then explain how the answer changes if rates fall, unemployment rises, property values drop, or the servicer stops advancing. MBS is not one risk. It is mortgage cash translated into security cash under rules.

Before approving a mortgage-backed security, draw two timelines. The first is the legal maturity written in the note documents. The second is the expected return of principal under slow, base, and fast prepayment. Then mark the dates on which defaults, recoveries, servicing advances, reserve draws, and trigger changes would alter investor cash. The difference between those timelines is where duration, reinvestment, extension, and liquidity risk become visible.
