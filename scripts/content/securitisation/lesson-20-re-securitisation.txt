A warehouse contains sealed boxes of machine parts. Instead of inspecting the parts, a buyer groups several boxes into a larger crate and sells claims on the crate. The new label creates another layer of packaging. It does not reveal whether the original boxes contain diverse parts or the same defective component.

Re-securitisation uses existing securitisation exposures as collateral or reference assets for a new securitisation. The investor depends on the performance of the original collateral, the first waterfall, the selected underlying tranches, and the second waterfall built around them.

Trace one mortgage payment through both wrappers. It enters the first mortgage pool, pays expenses and securities in the first layer, then contributes to cash received by an underlying tranche. That tranche is owned by a new vehicle. Its cash enters a second waterfall and supports another set of notes.

Every layer can reshape timing and loss. The first tranche may absorb loss only after junior mortgage claims are exhausted. The second transaction may then divide exposure to that tranche among new senior and junior notes. Understanding the top security requires looking through both capital structures.

The apparent diversification can be misleading. Ten underlying tranches may come from ten deals yet share the same mortgage vintages, regions, originators, servicers, underwriting standards, or housing-market driver. Deal count is not economic independence.

Correlation is especially dangerous because senior-looking underlying tranches can deteriorate together under a common stress. A model built on weak dependence assumptions may predict that very few reach their attachment points simultaneously. A nationwide shock can invalidate that comfort.

Information becomes thinner with distance. The re-securitisation investor may receive summary data about each underlying security rather than complete loan data. Changes in servicing, modification, triggers, and collateral performance must travel through multiple reporting layers.

Valuation also becomes model-dependent. Market prices for underlying tranches may be unavailable in stress. Expected cash requires assumptions about original loans, first waterfalls, triggers, correlations, recoveries, and the second waterfall. Small changes can move value sharply near attachment points.

Liquidity risk rises when the instrument is complex and holders disagree about value. A security can continue receiving some cash while its market becomes nearly impossible to trade at a reliable price. Credit performance and market liquidity are separate but reinforcing risks.

Re-securitisation is not inherently irrational. It can finance portfolios of existing securities, tailor risk positions, or manage holdings. The question is whether the second layer creates genuine diversification and transparent cash allocation or merely turns difficult assets into a harder label.

Suppose five underlying mezzanine tranches each attach after ten percent collateral loss. If their mortgage pools are genuinely independent, simultaneous impairment may be less likely. If they share one housing cycle and underwriting failure, the ten-percent barriers can fall together.

The second transaction's senior note is protected by its own subordination. That protection sits above underlying claims already shaped by subordination in the first layer. Stacking enhancement does not permit the analyst to add percentages mechanically; losses reach each layer through different balances and triggers.

Prepayment can travel through both layers as well. Faster mortgage principal changes first-tranche cash and expected life. The re-securitisation waterfall then reallocates that changed cash among notes in the second layer. Duration becomes a composition of borrower options and two contractual systems.

Counterparty and servicing concentrations can repeat across deals. Several underlying securities may rely on the same swap bank, trustee, servicer, or sponsor. A portfolio diversified by collateral identifiers can still contain a single operational point of failure.

The Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission documented the role of increasingly complex structured-credit products in the crisis, including securities built from other structured exposures. That history establishes the danger of opacity, correlation, and model dependence. It does not prove that every re-securitisation has the same collateral or outcome.

Securities disclosure frameworks can reveal underlying holdings and transaction terms. A current credit file needs position data, original deal documents, updated performance, waterfall results from the first layer, priority in the second layer, valuation assumptions, and concentration across common drivers.

Stress should begin at the loans, not at a haircut applied to the underlying tranche price. Change mortgage defaults, severity, timing, and prepayment. Run the first waterfalls. Feed resulting tranche cash into the second transaction. Only then observe the new notes.

If complete look-through data is unavailable, the limitation should reduce confidence and increase required margin rather than invite invented precision. Complexity does not become evidence because a model produces more decimal places.

Funds that hold other funds offer a clean parallel. The top portfolio may list twenty holdings, while those holdings own the same five companies. Counting wrappers overstates diversification. Looking through to the common economic exposures reveals the real concentration.

Re-securitisation places a waterfall on top of a waterfall. The second layer adds value only when it makes risk more usable without making its origin harder to see.
