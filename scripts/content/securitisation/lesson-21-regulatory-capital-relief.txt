A bank makes one hundred crore rupees of corporate loans. Those loans require funding, but they also consume regulatory capital because the bank bears credit risk. If the bank can transfer a meaningful portion of that risk, regulation may allow its capital requirement to fall. Moving an accounting balance alone is not enough.

Regulatory-capital-relief securitisation is designed to achieve recognised credit-risk transfer so the originating bank can reduce risk-weighted assets or associated capital requirements under the applicable framework. The transaction may involve asset sale, guarantees, credit-linked notes, or other permitted protection.

Follow one potential loan loss. Before transfer, the bank absorbs it through provisions, earnings, and capital. After effective risk transfer, a protection provider or securitisation investor absorbs a defined layer under the contract. Capital relief is credible only if the economic loss path and regulatory recognition point in the same direction.

Accounting derecognition asks whether assets leave the financial statements under accounting rules. Legal transfer asks who owns or is entitled to the assets. Regulatory capital recognition asks whether risk transfer meets prudential requirements. Economic transfer asks who actually loses money. The four tests can overlap without being identical.

Suppose a bank sells loans but guarantees nearly every loss and promises to repurchase weak assets without a narrow breach condition. Assets may appear elsewhere while risk remains economically with the bank. A supervisor will look beyond the sale label to recourse and retained exposure.

Now suppose the loans stay on the bank's balance sheet, but a funded credit-linked note absorbs losses between specified attachment and detachment points. Assets did not move. Credit risk may have moved sufficiently for capital purposes if the complete requirements are satisfied.

Significant risk transfer is the prudential idea that enough credit risk must leave the bank for capital relief to be justified. Exact tests depend on jurisdiction, transaction, and current rules. The principle is stable: cosmetic transfer should not release capital needed to protect the institution.

The bank may retain a first-loss position, a senior position, servicing duties, counterparty exposures, or residual claims. Retention can align incentives and comply with rules while reducing the amount of risk transferred. The retained pieces must be measured together rather than discussed one at a time.

Capital released has an opportunity cost and a destination. The bank may use it to support new lending, return capital, or change its portfolio. A transaction that releases capital but leaves concentrated tail risk can increase fragility if the bank immediately adds similar exposure.

Protection maturity matters. If credit protection expires before the reference loans, risk returns to the bank during the uncovered period. Maturity mismatch, early termination, call rights, and amortisation of protection can weaken recognition or economic effectiveness.

Counterparty risk matters in unfunded protection. A guarantee is useful only if the provider performs during the same stress that creates loan losses. Collateral, margining, eligible providers, downgrade provisions, and concentration affect the reliability of the transfer.

Basis risk appears when the protected reference, loss definition, or settlement method differs from the bank's actual exposure. A loan can suffer economic loss without satisfying the contractual credit event, or protection can pay an amount that does not match realised loss.

The cost of protection reduces the economics. Premiums, note coupons, fees, collateral costs, and retained risk must be compared with capital released and expected loss transfer. Capital relief is not free funding; it is a purchase of risk-bearing capacity under rules.

Regulatory arbitrage becomes a concern when a transaction targets capital reduction while transferring little real risk or exploiting gaps between measurement and economics. Good regulation attempts to align recognition with genuine loss transfer, but analysts should still inspect incentives.

Basel securitisation frameworks provide international prudential concepts, while national authorities implement applicable rules. Reserve Bank of India directions govern the Indian context. Exact capital treatment is date- and jurisdiction-specific and should be confirmed against current rules before execution.

A credit committee should request the reference portfolio, tranche boundaries, expected and stressed loss, retained exposures, protection terms, counterparties, maturity, settlement mechanics, accounting analysis, legal analysis, and regulatory approval or interpretation where required.

The capital bridge starts with risk-weighted assets and required capital before the transaction. It then shows recognised risk transfer, deductions, retained positions, transaction cost, and the resulting capital requirement. A closing-day benefit should also be recalculated after portfolio stress and protection amortisation.

Suppose the bank transfers a mezzanine loss band from three percent to ten percent. The bank retains the first three percent and losses above ten percent. The protection can remove a meaningful middle layer while leaving both frequent first loss and extreme tail risk with the bank.

Stress that arrangement by increasing correlation. Losses jump from modest to severe and pass rapidly through the protected band into the retained senior exposure. Capital relief based on ordinary loss expectations may still leave the bank exposed to a concentrated tail.

Reinsurance gives us a useful comparison. An insurer pays a premium so another balance sheet absorbs defined losses above a retention and below a limit. The insurer may receive capital benefit, but only within the contract, counterparty strength, and applicable solvency rules.

Regulatory capital relief is earned when legal form, economic loss, and prudential recognition agree. If one points elsewhere, the released capital may be an accounting shadow rather than transferred risk.
