No loan moves into the vehicle. Instead, one party pays a premium for protection on a reference portfolio, and another party promises to absorb defined credit events.

Synthetic CDO begins with a defined cash right, not a product name. The relevant collateral is reference credit exposures and protection contracts. That means the analyst must identify who owes the money, what contract creates the obligation, which account receives it, which party services or manages it, and what the investor can claim when the ordinary payment fails.

A synthetic CDO gains credit exposure through swaps or other protection contracts rather than owning the underlying loans. Premiums, collateral, attachment and detachment points, credit events, settlement, counterparty strength, and collateral investment create the cash path. The first useful model is a cash timeline: origination or purchase, scheduled collection, fees and permitted expenses, reserve or enhancement, note payment, principal allocation, and residual cash. If the timing changes, the investor’s return changes even when ultimate principal is eventually recovered.

A practical example makes the boundary visible. Imagine a pool with one hundred units of eligible collateral and notes supported by a defined portion of those collections. A payment arrives early, a payment arrives late, and a payment is disputed. The transaction documents decide whether each event changes principal, interest, reserve, enhancement, or only reporting. No single average can answer all three questions.

Historical synthetic structures show how correlation and counterparty risk can amplify losses. The source record supports the mechanism and the questions an investor should ask. It does not prove a new transaction’s pool performance, legal transfer, servicing quality, guarantee scope, or executable waterfall. Those missing facts remain diligence items rather than invitations to invent a conclusion.

The reference borrower can deteriorate without a settlement event. A protection seller can dispute or delay payment. Collateral can lose value, and a hedge can fail to match the exposure or settlement amount. The failure path matters because a shortfall can begin as borrower behaviour, asset condition, administrative error, market liquidity, legal challenge, counterparty weakness, or management choice. The same label can therefore contain different risks depending on the contract, jurisdiction, data, and operating party.

Initial collateral, margin, termination rights, counterparty eligibility, attachment points, and independent calculation provisions reduce different risks. None converts a reference exposure into a cash asset or removes basis risk. Protection should be described by the problem it solves. Credit enhancement absorbs value loss. Liquidity support bridges timing. A reserve supplies defined cash. Subordination changes loss priority. A guarantee transfers a claim to another counterparty. None is infinite, and none should be counted twice.

The analyst must ask who owns the loan, who references the loan, who pays the premium, what constitutes a credit event, how settlement works, and whether collateral remains available after counterparty failure. The interview-quality decision is to state the cash engine, the first failure bearer, the protection available, and the evidence still required. Ask what changes if the payment is early, late, reduced, redirected, or never arrives. Then ask whether the remedy is cash, time, legal enforcement, or only a revised forecast.

A disciplined review would build a small failure ledger for synthetic cdo. The first column names the payment source and legal obligor. The second records the servicing, management, collection, or collateral process that must work. The third identifies the test, reserve, guarantee, or priority rule that responds. The fourth states what the investor actually receives after delay, expense, loss, or counterparty failure. This keeps a clean balance-sheet description from being mistaken for a cash-flow conclusion.

The same ledger should carry the limit of the evidence. Pool data can support performance analysis; a legal opinion can support a transfer question; a trustee report can support a payment history; an offering document can define a test. None of those documents silently proves the others. For synthetic cdo, the most important unanswered question is the one that changes who controls cash when the ordinary route stops working.
