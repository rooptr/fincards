A warehouse contains high-quality goods, but every trucker refuses to carry them because funding for the journey has vanished. The goods did not spoil overnight. The route between buyer and seller froze. Asset-backed markets can suffer the same separation between collateral performance and financing liquidity.

During the financial crisis, investors became unwilling or unable to finance many asset-backed securities even when consumer loans continued producing cash. Falling market confidence, uncertain valuation, and scarce leverage reduced new issuance and trading. Borrowers and securities were connected through a funding market that had stopped functioning normally.

The United States Federal Reserve announced the Term Asset-Backed Securities Loan Facility, known as T A L F, in November two thousand eight. The facility was designed to support the issuance of asset-backed securities backed by eligible consumer and small-business credit by providing term funding to eligible investors against qualifying collateral.

Follow one eligible security. An investor purchases it and pledges it as collateral for a T A L F loan under the facility terms. The loan supplies financing. The security's borrower cash still supports its payments. Public liquidity improves the investor's ability to hold the asset; it does not replace the underlying loans.

The financing was described as non-recourse subject to the program terms. If the borrower under the facility surrendered the collateral and satisfied applicable conditions, recourse was limited rather than becoming an unlimited claim on every investor asset. That feature made the financing useful while placing collateral risk and haircuts at the centre.

A haircut means the facility lends less than the collateral's value under the applicable schedule. If a security worth one hundred receives financing of ninety, the investor supplies ten of equity. The haircut protects the lender against valuation and loss, but it is not a prediction that loss will stop at ten.

Eligibility matters because the facility was not an open promise to finance any structured product. Asset class, rating, issuance, maturity, documentation, and other program conditions determined what could be pledged under the relevant terms.

That boundary separates market support from a blanket guarantee. A qualifying security could receive funding access while still carrying borrower default, servicing, prepayment, recovery, model, and structural risk. Eligibility supported a channel; it did not certify investment success.

Suppose auto-loan borrowers continue paying, but private lenders demand extreme haircuts on the securities. Investors sell to raise cash, prices fall, and new auto-loan securitisations become uneconomic. Funding stress can reduce credit availability even before collateral losses rise.

Term funding interrupts that spiral by giving eligible investors a more stable borrowing horizon against qualifying securities. Demand can return, issuance can resume, and lenders can finance new consumer credit. The mechanism works through market liquidity and balance-sheet capacity.

Now suppose the underlying auto loans actually default at severe rates. T A L F financing does not make those payments reappear. Collateral losses reduce security value, consume enhancement, and can reach the investor's equity and the protected financing arrangement according to program terms.

Haircut and credit enhancement operate at different layers. Enhancement sits inside the asset-backed security and protects its notes from collateral loss. The facility haircut sits between the pledged security's value and the public loan. Both provide cushions, but they absorb different exposures.

Prepayment adds timing risk even when credit remains strong. Faster borrower principal can shorten the security and alter reinvestment. Slow principal can extend it. Facility maturity and collateral maturity must be considered together so funding does not end while the security remains unexpectedly long.

Valuation is difficult in a frozen market. Low transaction prices may reflect expected credit loss, liquidity premium, forced selling, or uncertainty. A lending facility requires rules for collateral valuation and haircuts without pretending every market quote reflects fundamental cash value.

The Federal Reserve announcement establishes T A L F's purpose and core mechanism in the United States crisis setting. Later program terms and operations add detail. The historical facility should not be described as an Indian rule or as a permanent feature available on unchanged terms.

Securities disclosures remain necessary because public financing cannot substitute for understanding collateral, servicing, waterfall, enhancement, maturity, and risks. The facility can improve market functioning while private investors still decide whether the security is worth owning.

The program also creates public-risk questions. Haircuts, eligibility, collateral valuation, Treasury protection, and Federal Reserve lending terms determine how loss is shared. Liquidity policy becomes credible when the public balance sheet supports market function without denying that collateral can fail.

Compare two problems. In the first, borrowers are paying but investors cannot finance sound securities. Liquidity support can be powerful. In the second, borrowers are defaulting and recoveries are collapsing. Funding may slow forced sales, but credit loss remains.

Now combine them. Collateral deteriorates while market liquidity disappears. Prices fall for both fundamental and funding reasons. A decision-maker must separate expected cash loss, risk premium, haircut, forced-sale discount, and duration rather than assigning the entire price decline to one cause.

Imagine instead a private warehouse lender funding solar-loan securities in India. Stable term funding could prevent short-term refinancing pressure. It would not guarantee household payments or panel recovery. The jurisdiction and provider changed; the line between liquidity and credit did not.

T A L F demonstrated that a market can need help carrying assets even when the assets still produce cash. Liquidity can reopen the road. Only collateral performance determines what ultimately arrives at the destination.
