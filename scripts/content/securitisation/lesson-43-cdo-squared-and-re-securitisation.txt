An investor buys a slice of many securitisations and receives a new note. The second structure looks diversified, but each underlying slice may already contain the same mortgages, loans, or corporate risks.

CDO-Squared and Re-Securitisation begins with a defined cash right, not a product name. The relevant collateral is tranches of earlier securitisations. That means the analyst must identify who owes the money, what contract creates the obligation, which account receives it, which party services or manages it, and what the investor can claim when the ordinary payment fails.

A CDO-squared or re-securitisation pools tranches or structured claims rather than directly originated loans. The new waterfall depends on the first deals’ waterfalls, correlation, cash timing, tranche thickness, servicer performance, and the second vehicle’s priority rules. The first useful model is a cash timeline: origination or purchase, scheduled collection, fees and permitted expenses, reserve or enhancement, note payment, principal allocation, and residual cash. If the timing changes, the investor’s return changes even when ultimate principal is eventually recovered.

A practical example makes the boundary visible. Imagine a pool with one hundred units of eligible collateral and notes supported by a defined portion of those collections. A payment arrives early, a payment arrives late, and a payment is disputed. The transaction documents decide whether each event changes principal, interest, reserve, enhancement, or only reporting. No single average can answer all three questions.

Offering documents, tranche reports, collateral composition, correlation assumptions, and the historical FCIC record help separate legal layers from economic exposures. Public descriptions rarely provide enough look-through data for a current conclusion. The source record supports the mechanism and the questions an investor should ask. It does not prove a new transaction’s pool performance, legal transfer, servicing quality, guarantee scope, or executable waterfall. Those missing facts remain diligence items rather than invitations to invent a conclusion.

Nested seniority can make a claim appear remote from loss while common underlying collateral creates simultaneous deterioration. A delay or modelling error in the first deal becomes an input into the second deal’s payment and test calculations. The failure path matters because a shortfall can begin as borrower behaviour, asset condition, administrative error, market liquidity, legal challenge, counterparty weakness, or management choice. The same label can therefore contain different risks depending on the contract, jurisdiction, data, and operating party.

Subordination, overcollateralisation, triggers, reserves, diversification limits, and independent calculation can absorb or reveal stress. A second layer of protection is not the same as a second independent pool of cash. Protection should be described by the problem it solves. Credit enhancement absorbs value loss. Liquidity support bridges timing. A reserve supplies defined cash. Subordination changes loss priority. A guarantee transfers a claim to another counterparty. None is infinite, and none should be counted twice.

The analyst should look through every tranche to the borrower and property exposures below it, then trace timing and loss allocation through both waterfalls. If look-through is impossible, that is an investment fact, not a missing footnote. The interview-quality decision is to state the cash engine, the first failure bearer, the protection available, and the evidence still required. Ask what changes if the payment is early, late, reduced, redirected, or never arrives. Then ask whether the remedy is cash, time, legal enforcement, or only a revised forecast.

A disciplined review would build a small failure ledger for cdo-squared and re-securitisation. The first column names the payment source and legal obligor. The second records the servicing, management, collection, or collateral process that must work. The third identifies the test, reserve, guarantee, or priority rule that responds. The fourth states what the investor actually receives after delay, expense, loss, or counterparty failure. This keeps a clean balance-sheet description from being mistaken for a cash-flow conclusion.

The same ledger should carry the limit of the evidence. Pool data can support performance analysis; a legal opinion can support a transfer question; a trustee report can support a payment history; an offering document can define a test. None of those documents silently proves the others. For cdo-squared and re-securitisation, the most important unanswered question is the one that changes who controls cash when the ordinary route stops working.

Re-securitisation is not automatically wrong; it can redistribute risk. But each additional layer adds model, correlation, data, legal, and governance dependencies. The concept travels only when the learner can separate a transferable right from the business, asset, manager, platform, or public rule that keeps producing it.

A nested structure does not erase the first pool; it multiplies the places where a first-pool mistake can become a second-pool loss.
