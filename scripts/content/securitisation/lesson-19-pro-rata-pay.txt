Two friends lend money to the same shop. When the shop repays principal, it can pay the first friend completely before paying the second, or reduce both loans together in agreed proportions. The total cash is unchanged. What changes is how quickly the first lender's protection from the second lender grows.

Pro rata pay allocates available principal among eligible note classes according to stated proportions or balances. Sequential pay directs principal to senior classes first. The choice changes amortisation, expected life, subordination, and the point at which losses can reach each investor.

Suppose senior notes begin at eighty crore rupees and junior notes at twenty crore. The pool also begins at one hundred crore. Ten crore of principal arrives. Under an eighty-twenty pro rata rule, senior receives eight and junior receives two. Their balances become seventy-two and eighteen.

Under sequential pay, all ten crore retires senior notes. Senior falls to seventy while junior remains twenty. The junior balance beneath senior is now a larger share of the outstanding notes. Sequential payment has built senior protection faster.

Pro rata payment is not automatically reckless. Returning principal to both classes can match the collateral's planned amortisation, reduce unnecessary extension for junior investors, and avoid leaving a small senior note outstanding against a shrinking pool. It becomes dangerous when protection releases before performance has been proven.

Eligibility tests govern whether pro rata payment continues. The transaction may require minimum enhancement, acceptable delinquencies, cumulative losses below a threshold, reserve compliance, and no servicer or payment event. Healthy performance earns shared amortisation.

A switch to sequential pay protects senior investors after deterioration. Once a trigger fails, future principal stops reaching junior notes and concentrates on senior reduction. Some switches are permanent; others can cure after specified conditions and periods.

The cure rule changes incentives and value. A temporary breach with automatic cure can delay junior cash for a short period. A permanent switch can reshape the remaining life even if collateral later improves. The threshold alone does not describe the consequence.

Now imagine junior notes amortise rapidly during two healthy years. A recession begins in year three. Fewer junior claims remain to absorb loss. Pro rata payment has released protection before the stress arrived. Whether that was acceptable depends on the trigger design and original assumptions.

Overcollateralisation tests can prevent that release. Required asset coverage is measured against note balances. If coverage falls toward a floor, principal or excess cash can be redirected until the required cushion is restored.

Prepayment interacts with the rule. Fast principal can return junior capital quickly under pro rata allocation. The same fast principal can build senior subordination rapidly under sequential allocation. An assumption presented as collateral timing becomes a capital-structure assumption through the waterfall.

Defaults interact in the opposite direction. Collateral balance falls without equivalent note repayment when principal is lost. Enhancement shrinks. A pro rata test calculated on stale or generous measures may continue releasing junior principal while protection is already eroding.

Interest should not be confused with principal allocation. A junior note may continue receiving interest while its principal is locked out, or interest may defer after a trigger. The exact priority determines whether the switch changes only amortisation or current coupon as well.

Suppose the senior note has a short expected life under sequential pay and a longer life under pro rata pay. Investors will price not only credit loss but duration and liquidity. A safer loss position can still carry an unwanted extension profile.

Public transaction disclosures can describe principal-allocation mechanics and trigger tests. Reserve Bank of India requirements provide applicable regulatory boundaries. The investment conclusion requires the executed priority, current balances, collateral performance, and a model that changes state correctly.

Review the percentage calculation carefully. Pro rata can mean original note percentages, current note percentages, targeted balances, or another formula. Using the wrong denominator redirects principal even when every input number is correct.

An analyst should run at least three paths: continued pro rata payment, immediate sequential switch, and delayed deterioration after substantial junior amortisation. The third path often reveals the risk hidden by a healthy current trigger.

Move the example to two layers of sandbags. Removing both layers together as the river recedes may be efficient. If the forecast is wrong and the river rises again, the inner wall has lost part of its protection. A switch rule decides when removal must stop.

Pro rata pay is controlled release of subordination over time. Its safety depends less on the elegance of the percentage than on whether the transaction stops releasing protection before the loss arrives.
