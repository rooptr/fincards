A group of apartment owners appoints one manager to collect rent. After permitted expenses, each owner receives a proportionate share of whatever rent actually arrived that month. A tenant who pays early changes the timing. A vacant apartment reduces the total. The manager distributes rather than promising a separate fixed repayment schedule.

A pass-through security follows the same economic idea. Collateral principal and interest, after specified deductions and adjustments, pass through to investors according to their ownership interests. Investors remain closely exposed to the timing of the underlying asset cash flows.

Take one ordinary mortgage payment. The borrower pays scheduled interest and principal. The servicer collects it, deducts or reports permitted amounts, and remits available cash. The transaction distributes the investor's share. The security does not detach that payment from the borrower's timing merely because it packages many loans together.

Pooling smooths individual noise. One borrower paying early matters little inside thousands of mortgages. Widespread refinancing matters greatly. Granularity reduces dependence on one account; it does not remove common behaviour across the pool.

Prepayment is the defining timing risk for many pass-through investors. When borrowers refinance or sell homes, principal returns sooner than scheduled. The investor receives money, so credit has not failed. Yet future interest disappears and reinvestment happens at whatever market rate is then available.

When rates fall, refinancing often accelerates. Investors may receive principal precisely when comparable new investments offer lower yields. When rates rise, prepayment can slow and principal remains outstanding longer than expected. The security can shorten in an unattractive market and extend in another unattractive market.

That reversal is called negative convexity in mortgage analysis. The term describes how borrower options can make price appreciation limited when rates fall and price decline more severe when rates rise. Understanding the cash behaviour comes before the label: borrowers choose when to return principal.

Scheduled principal also changes over time. An amortising loan pays more principal as it ages under many standard schedules. Pool age, remaining term, loan rate, borrower incentives, and burnout all influence projected cash.

Servicing fees and other permitted deductions reduce gross borrower collections before distribution. An investor does not own the borrower's full coupon merely because the mortgage carries that rate. The pass-through rate reflects the amounts available after contractual deductions.

Delinquency complicates timing. Some arrangements use servicing advances to maintain scheduled distributions when payments are late, subject to recoverability. The investor may temporarily receive cash that the borrower has not supplied. Advances alter timing; eventual default and recovery still determine value.

Default removes scheduled cash and introduces recovery. Foreclosure, property sale, insurance, guarantees, legal cost, and delay shape the amount passed through later. Direct exposure to collateral timing includes the unpleasant paths as well as ordinary instalments.

A pass-through arrangement can distribute cash pro rata among holders of one class. More complex transactions may create different classes or support around pass-through interests. The name alone therefore cannot reveal every priority, guarantee, or timing feature in the complete deal.

Suppose a pool receives one crore rupees of interest and two crore of principal. After servicing and permitted expenses, net interest and principal are distributed under the governing certificates. If prepayments add another three crore of principal, investor principal jumps that month even though scheduled amortisation did not.

The weighted average life changes with that additional principal. A model should show multiple prepayment speeds, not one confident forecast. The Public Securities Association benchmark, commonly called P S A, provides a convention for comparing mortgage prepayment assumptions. It is not a promise about this pool's borrowers.

Reserve Bank of India securitisation directions recognise pass-through certificates and relevant transaction requirements in the Indian market. Applicable documents define how collections, deductions, credit enhancement, and investor rights work in a specific issuance. The regulatory category does not remove the need to read the cash-allocation terms.

An investor needs pool balance, loan rates, remaining terms, seasoning, delinquency, prepayment history, servicing practices, advance policy, fees, enhancement, and distribution timing. A yield quoted without the assumed payment speed is incomplete because the speed changes both cash dates and reinvestment.

Now let a group of borrowers prepay after property prices rise. The pool balance falls, senior exposure may reduce, and future default exposure disappears on those loans. At the same time, the remaining pool can become more concentrated in borrowers unable or unwilling to refinance. Selection changes as principal leaves.

Equipment leases show the structure outside mortgages. They distribute net rentals to certificate holders. Early lease termination, equipment sale, delayed rent, and maintenance deductions alter the passed cash. The underlying asset changed, but the investor still lives close to the timing of actual collections.

A pass-through security offers transparency by keeping the investor near the collateral stream. That closeness is not immunity. It means the borrower owns much of the clock, and the investor receives the consequences.
