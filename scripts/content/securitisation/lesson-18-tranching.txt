Three people stand behind three walls of different thickness while stones fall from the same roof. The first wall absorbs the earliest impact, the second is reached only after the first fails, and the third is protected longest. The roof did not become stronger. Exposure to its failure was placed in an order.

Tranching divides securities backed by one collateral pool into classes with different payment priority, loss position, maturity, and sometimes control rights. Senior, mezzanine, junior, and residual claims experience the same underlying assets through different contractual boundaries.

Suppose a one-hundred-crore-rupee pool supports seventy crore of senior notes, twenty crore of mezzanine notes, and a ten-crore residual position. If collateral suffers four crore of principal loss and the documents allocate loss from the bottom upward, the residual absorbs it. The senior pool did not avoid loss; another claim stood in front of it.

Subordination is the amount of lower-ranking capital available beneath a tranche. The senior notes in this example begin with thirty crore of claims below them, subject to the complete enhancement and waterfall terms. Subordination can protect against loss and sometimes payment interruption, but its availability changes as balances, losses, and distributions move.

The attachment point marks the collateral-loss boundary at which a tranche begins taking loss. The detachment point marks the boundary at which that tranche is exhausted. A tranche attaching after ten percent and detaching after thirty percent is exposed to the loss band between those points.

Those percentages are not fixed by vocabulary. Amortisation can change tranche balances. Release of overcollateralisation can reduce support. Sequential principal can build senior protection. Pro rata principal can return junior capital while senior notes remain outstanding. The capital stack has motion.

Payment priority and loss priority reinforce each other. Senior interest and principal are generally paid before junior claims, while losses are generally allocated from junior toward senior. The senior holder receives cash earlier and deterioration later; the residual holder receives optional upside later and deterioration first.

This asymmetry can create securities suited to different investors. A conservative investor may prefer a senior claim with substantial subordination. A specialist may buy mezzanine risk for higher yield. A sponsor may retain residual economics. Suitability does not mean the pool's total risk has vanished.

Expected loss combines probability of default, exposure, severity, timing, and the tranche's position. A senior tranche can have low expected loss while still carrying extension, liquidity, model, legal, and tail risk. Credit rating and investment return address different dimensions.

Suppose defaults are dispersed and recoveries arrive quickly. The residual absorbs modest loss and senior notes pay as expected. Now correlate defaults through one housing downturn. Losses cross the junior boundary faster, recoveries weaken together, and mezzanine protection disappears. Tranching allocates correlation; it does not neutralise it.

Excess spread can absorb loss before funded tranches are written down. A reserve can provide cash. Overcollateralisation creates assets beyond note balances. Guarantees and insurance add counterparty promises. Each form of enhancement has its own legal mechanics, timing, and failure mode.

An investor should never add every protection into one percentage without checking overlap. A reserve funded from overcollateralisation may not be independent. Excess spread may vanish in the same scenario that increases loss. A guarantee is only as reliable as its provider and conditions.

Tranche thickness is the distance between attachment and detachment. A thick tranche can absorb more loss within its band than a thin tranche. Thickness alone says nothing about the probability that collateral reaches the attachment point.

Now let senior principal amortise from seventy crore to forty crore while junior balances remain. The percentage of protection beneath senior can rise substantially. If junior principal amortises alongside senior, the protection may not build. Principal allocation therefore changes future loss exposure before any new default occurs.

Triggers can stop junior amortisation, trap residual cash, or switch pro rata payment to sequential. Those clauses preserve protection when performance weakens. Their effectiveness depends on thresholds, measurement dates, cure rules, and how early deterioration becomes visible.

The financial crisis showed that highly rated structured claims could still suffer when mortgage losses, correlation, model assumptions, and market liquidity deteriorated beyond expectations. The Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission provides evidence about those mechanisms. It does not mean every senior tranche failed or that ratings contain no information.

Reserve Bank of India directions and securities disclosure frameworks require careful treatment of tranche characteristics, retention, enhancement, and payment terms in applicable contexts. A regulatory category or rating cannot replace calculation of how collateral loss reaches a specific claim.

The calculation begins with the pool, not the note label. Project collateral defaults, severity, recovery timing, prepayment, expenses, and interest. Run those cash flows through the actual waterfall. Observe when each tranche misses interest, extends, writes down, or recovers.

Imagine an investor offered a senior note with twenty percent enhancement. If the pool is granular, seasoned, well-serviced auto loans, that number has one meaning. If the pool is correlated distressed property claims with delayed recoveries, the same number has another.

Transfer the model to flood protection. Several barriers can protect the town in sequence. Calling the inner barrier strong means little without the expected water level, the strength of outer barriers, and whether gates remain closed as the storm develops.

Tranching can make one claim safer only by making another claim absorb risk sooner. The transaction rearranges the fall; it never repairs the roof.
