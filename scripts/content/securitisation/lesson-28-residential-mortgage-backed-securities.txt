A couple in Pune buys an apartment with a twenty-year mortgage. The bank records their income, verifies the property, checks the down payment, and expects a monthly instalment. Years later, the loan may sit inside a residential mortgage-backed security. The investor now needs to understand not just whether the couple pays, but how thousands of households behave together when rates, jobs, house prices, and servicing practices change.

Residential mortgage-backed securities, or R M B S, are securities supported primarily by residential mortgage loans. The defining collateral is the household mortgage, but the real risk is a distribution of borrower income, property value, documentation, payment behaviour, refinancing choices, geography, and legal recovery. Two pools with the same average loan-to-value can have very different cash flows if one is seasoned and geographically diverse while the other is new and concentrated in one employment market.

Underwriting is the first hidden variable. Income verification, debt obligations, employment history, credit profile, property occupancy, loan purpose, documentation quality, and loan-to-value all influence the chance that scheduled cash arrives. A low loan-to-value can provide recovery cushion, but it cannot pay the instalment when the borrower loses income. A strong property can support recovery, but foreclosure takes time and costs money.

Imagine a pool with one hundred crore rupees of principal. Scheduled interest and principal arrive each month. Some borrowers refinance and repay early. Some move and sell. Some fall thirty days late, cure, and return to current status. Others roll into deeper delinquency, request modification, or default. The servicer's roll-rate report is more informative than one lifetime default percentage because it shows how the population is changing.

Prepayment creates an option held by the homeowner. When rates fall, refinancing can accelerate principal return. The investor may receive cash sooner but lose high-coupon interest and face reinvestment at lower rates. When rates rise, prepayments can slow, the security extends, and the investor remains exposed to mortgage credit and duration for longer. The same security can therefore be paid early in a falling-rate market and remain outstanding in a rising-rate market.

The 2008 U.S. mortgage crisis is evidence about mechanism, not permission to generalise carelessly. The Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission documented weak underwriting, incentive conflict, poor information, and the way mortgage losses travelled through structured products. R M B S analysis must use that history to ask what was verified, who retained exposure, how representations could be enforced, how servicing changed after transfer, and whether the model stressed correlated house-price and employment shocks.

Loss does not move directly from a foreclosed house to every noteholder. The servicer records the default, advances or does not advance permitted amounts, manages the property, receives sale proceeds, deducts costs, and sends the net amount through the waterfall. Excess spread, reserve funds, overcollateralisation, mortgage insurance, subordination, and guarantees may absorb different portions at different times. Their legal mechanics must be read separately.

Agency and non-agency R M B S make a useful comparison. An agency structure can provide a defined guarantee or credit support under the relevant program, changing the investor's exposure to borrower credit loss. It does not automatically remove prepayment, interest-rate, liquidity, servicing, or operational risk. A non-agency structure relies more heavily on pool quality and funded enhancement. The distinction is a contract, not a marketing adjective.

An Indian bridge must remain precise. RBI loan-transfer and securitisation directions establish requirements for eligible loan pools, transfer, retention, and transaction conduct. They do not supply the borrower data data for a hypothetical R M B S. To make a transaction conclusion, an analyst still needs the dated loan tape, underwriting fields, delinquency history, modification data, property information, servicing report, legal transfer analysis, and executable waterfall.

The interview test is simple to state and difficult to fake. What happens to investor cash if refinancing doubles, unemployment rises, house prices fall, and the servicer loses the records? A strong answer separates frequency, severity, timing, legal enforceability, enhancement consumption, and market liquidity. A weak answer says the pool is residential and therefore diversified.

R M B S is the study of household promises translated through property, servicing, law, and time. The investor is protected only while the borrower cash, the property recovery, the documents, and the waterfall continue to tell the same story.

Do not let a clean vintage report close the analysis. Ask whether the borrower mix changed, whether underwriting standards loosened, whether modification policies altered roll rates, whether the servicer advanced interest, whether insurance proceeds were available, and whether the geography shares one employment or property shock. Residential granularity helps, but thousands of households can still move together when the same rates, wages, lenders, or housing market affect them.
